\begin{abstract}
Advances in air-defense interception increasingly threaten the survivability of offensive unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs), making cooperative swarm penetration important for defeating complex defensive systems.
To address the low survivability and limited penetration capability of offensive UAV swarms under cooperative interception, this paper proposes an intelligent cooperative maneuvering penetration method that dynamically reconfigures swarm tasks while accounting for interceptor field-of-view (FOV) constraints.
First, a safety-margin evaluation model is developed from interceptor detection range, FOV geometry, 
and relative offensive--defensive motion to characterize the detection risk to each offensive UAV under overlapping multi-interceptor coverage. 
Second, based on the evaluated risk and group penetration benefit, an entropy-regularized potential game updates each UAV's task-probability distribution online among decoy-containment, 
concealment-evasion, and primary-attack tasks without preset assignments. The reconfigured task probabilities are then incorporated into centralized value estimation and task-conditioned decentralized policy execution through a task-trust attention multi-agent proximal policy optimization method, while trust feedback regulates policy exploration. 
Hardware-in-the-loop simulation results indicate that the proposed method adapts the swarm's cooperative strategy to the evolving interception geometry, disrupts interceptor--target assignments through decoy maneuvers, coordinates multiple UAVs to penetrate the defensive formation, and increases the probability of successful penetration.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

As interception technologies and equipment continue to advance, a single UAV can no longer provide sufficient penetration capability in complex defensive environments. Cooperative penetration by UAV swarms has therefore become increasingly important.
By overlapping the detection fields of view (FOVs) of multiple interceptors, the defender can establish a tightly coupled interception geometry. 
Independent evasive maneuvers and preset task assignments among offensive UAVs are generally insufficient to defeat such cooperative interception.
The resulting challenge is to develop intelligent guidance methods that dynamically reconfigure swarm tasks and exploit coordinated maneuvers to create viable penetration opportunities.


Existing UAV penetration methods can be broadly classified as saturation penetration or maneuvering penetration.
Saturation penetration mainly relies on coordinated attacks by multiple UAVs in terms of attack time and attack angle, thereby stressing the firepower allocation and interception resources of the defense system within limited reaction time. For multi-missile salvo attacks, Zhao et al.\cite{ZhaoZhou2008AAS} proposed a cooperative multi-missile guidance structure based on coordination variables, linking the remaining flight time with guidance commands and laying a foundation for distributed penetration attacks. Zhao et al.\cite{ZhaoYang2017AAS} systematically reviewed the development of cooperative multi-missile guidance in time coordination and angle coordination, and pointed out that the core of cooperative guidance is to transform individual terminal constraints into group-consensus constraints. Zhao et al.\cite{Zhao2015CJA} further designed three-dimensional cooperative guidance laws for stationary and maneuvering targets by adding a coordination term to proportional navigation, enabling multiple missiles to arrive simultaneously under centralized or distributed information conditions. Zeng et al.\cite{Zeng2018CJA} combined midcourse Dubins path planning with a terminal cooperative guidance law to realize full-process coordination from trajectory generation to terminal simultaneous attack. To enhance spatiotemporal coordination in saturation penetration, Wang et al.\cite{WangLu2018ISA} studied a distributed guidance law with three-dimensional impact-angle constraints, allowing multiple missiles to form specified incidence angles under local information. Lyu et al.\cite{Lyu2019AST} realized cooperative simultaneous attack of multiple missiles under directed communication topology, reflecting the influence of communication constraints on cooperative-behavior stability. Jiang et al.\cite{Jiang2018AST} used convex optimization to handle cooperative guidance with multiple constraints, integrating path, acceleration, time, and terminal-angle requirements into a receding-horizon optimization framework and improving solvability in complex constrained scenarios. Chen et al.\cite{Chen2021AST} proposed a three-dimensional fixed-time robust cooperative guidance law, constructing consensus variables from range-to-go and radial relative velocity to achieve rapid convergence of attack time and impact angle while avoiding excessive reliance on remaining-flight-time estimation. Wang et al.\cite{Ai2019FOVSalvo} introduced seeker FOV constraints into a two-stage three-dimensional salvo guidance design, coordinating attack time and attack angle while satisfying FOV boundaries.

Maneuvering penetration reduces the probabilities of detection, target acquisition, and successful interception by actively modifying the flight trajectory and line-of-sight geometry. Early studies usually characterized the penetration mechanism from the perspectives of pursuit--evasion games and optimal control. Shinar\cite{Shinar1977JA} analyzed optimal evasive maneuvers using a two-dimensional linearized kinematic model and showed that interceptor acceleration-command saturation and the roll capability of the offensive UAV significantly affect the optimal maneuver sequence and final miss distance. Shinar et al.\cite{Shinar1979JGC} extended the evasion problem to three dimensions, transformed optimal evasion into a roll-attitude control problem, and emphasized the effects of escape-plane selection, maximum-acceleration use, and roll-rate constraints on penetration performance. For engineering implementation, Karelahti et al.\cite{Karelahti2007JGCD} proposed a receding-horizon control framework to generate near-optimal missile-avoidance trajectories online. Considering UAV pitch and roll-rate constraints and a missile proportional-navigation model, they compared the effects of miss distance, closing velocity, seeker angular rate, and control energy on evasion strategy. For hypersonic glide vehicles, Zhu et al.\cite{Zhu2018AST} proposed a pendulum maneuvering penetration strategy that integrates impact-point prediction error, interception velocity, maneuver acceleration, and energy consumption into a penetration performance index, reflecting the idea of disrupting defender trajectory prediction through lateral maneuvers. Shen et al.\cite{Shen2022AST} studied penetration trajectory optimization for a hypersonic glide vehicle encountering two interceptors, transformed the nonconvex optimal-control problem into sequential second-order cone programming, and generated flyable trajectories considering both target-region and evasion constraints. Gao et al.\cite{Gao2025AAS} further considered interceptor detection-capability limitations and studied intelligent maneuvering penetration guidance, extending maneuvering penetration from predesigned trajectories to situation-driven online decision making. For common terminal guidance constraints such as impact angle and seeker FOV limits, He and Lin\cite{He2015FOV} designed a robust guidance law considering both impact-angle constraints and seeker FOV boundaries, showing that FOV boundaries, line-of-sight angle variations, and terminal-angle constraints directly affect the achievable guidance commands and acquisition regions of both sides. With the development of hypersonic vehicles and swarm offensive--defensive missions, Yu et al.\cite{Yu2020CJA} proposed a cooperative penetration guidance strategy for multiple hypersonic glide vehicles that combines individual evasion with group cooperation. Chen et al.\cite{Chen2024CJA} established a cooperative game penetration guidance method for multiple hypersonic vehicles under a safety-critical framework, and further considered performance constraints to form an adaptive cooperative game penetration strategy\cite{Chen2024TAES}, extending maneuvering penetration from geometric evasion to offensive--defensive payoff confrontation and safety-constraint maintenance.

Data-driven intelligent cooperative penetration methods learn from large volumes of data and reduce dependence on specific assumptions; they are becoming a new research trend.
Deep reinforcement learning provides methods for autonomous decision making in complex offensive--defensive environments. Single-agent deep reinforcement learning mainly approximates the policy function through deep neural networks to improve decision-making capability in high-dimensional state spaces\cite{Mnih2015Nature,Schulman2017PPO}.
For multi-agent cooperative tasks, existing studies mainly focus on centralized training with decentralized execution\cite{Lowe2017MADDPG,Yu2022MAPPO}, team-value decomposition\cite{Sunehag2018VDN,Rashid2020JMLR}, credit assignment\cite{Foerster2018COMA}, and attention-based information interaction\cite{Iqbal2019MAAC}, to alleviate environmental nonstationarity, incomplete local observation, and difficulty in global-reward allocation during multi-agent training.
In penetration guidance applications, Gaudet et al.\cite{Gaudet2020AST} used reinforcement learning for angle-only intercept guidance of maneuvering targets, showing that learned policies can approximate effective guidance behavior under observation limitations. Liu et al.\cite{Liu2021AST} combined deep-network remaining-time prediction with a reinforcement-learning correction term to solve prediction-correction guidance for impact-time control. Qiu et al.\cite{Qiu2022JAE} established deep-reinforcement-learning strategies for ballistic-missile maneuvering penetration and treated the penetration process as a continuous adversarial decision problem. Yan et al.\cite{Yan2022SciRep} proposed a hierarchical reinforcement learning method for missile evasion and guidance, using high- and low-level policies to handle complex maneuvering tasks. Hui et al.\cite{Hui2025CJA} proposed a multi-missile coordinated penetration strategy based on hierarchical reinforcement learning in a reduced-dimensional space, using penetration-corridor constraints to reduce the search dimension and improve cooperative maneuvering efficiency. Qiu and Gao\cite{Qiu2024AST} proposed a value--policy decomposed multi-agent reinforcement learning method for three-dimensional time-cooperative guidance with impact-angle constraints, to alleviate reward coupling and policy coupling in three-dimensional cooperative guidance. Chen et al.\cite{Chen2024CJA,Chen2024TAES} established cooperative game penetration guidance frameworks under safety-critical and performance-constrained conditions for multiple hypersonic vehicles, reflecting the development from single-vehicle evasion to multi-vehicle cooperative game decision making.

In summary, time- and angle-coordinated methods can improve swarm penetration, but they do not adequately support maneuvering evasion under cooperative interception by multiple interceptors. Cooperative mechanisms that use decoy and cover maneuvers to disrupt interceptor--target assignments are therefore required.

To address the low penetration success rate of offensive UAV swarms under multi-interceptor cooperative interception, this paper develops an intelligent cooperative penetration guidance method. A safety-margin model quantifies UAV detection risk, a group-game mechanism dynamically reconfigures penetration tasks, and a multi-agent reinforcement learning framework generates maneuver commands online. The principal contributions are as follows:

1) A safety-margin evaluation model combines detection-FOV constraints with relative offensive--defensive motion to characterize the detection risk to offensive UAVs under overlapping FOV coverage from multiple interceptors.

2) An entropy-regularized potential-game mechanism dynamically reconfigures cooperative tasks without preset assignments, enabling the swarm to combine decoy-containment, concealment-evasion, and primary-attack penetration.

3) A trust-exploration-driven multi-agent proximal policy optimization method incorporates the dynamic-task-reconfiguration prior into both centralized value estimation and task-conditioned decentralized policy execution, improving policy convergence and the stability of online guidance decisions in complex adversarial environments.

\section{Cooperative UAV-Swarm Penetration Modeling under Multi-Interceptor FOV Constraints}
\label{sec:aircraft-model}

Let $\mathcal{A}=\{A_1,A_2,\dots,A_{N_A}\}$ and $\mathcal{D}=\{D_1,D_2,\dots,D_{N_I}\}$ denote the offensive and interceptor swarms. In the asymmetric HVT engagement of Fig.~\ref{fig:engagement-geometry}, $V_j^{\max}>V_i^{\max}$ and $a_n^{D,\max}>a_n^{A,\max}$ for $A_i\in\mathcal A$ and $D_j\in\mathcal D$.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/gao1_V5.jpg}
  \caption{Schematic of cooperative penetration operations by a UAV swarm}
  \label{fig:engagement-geometry}
\end{figure}

Let $\mathcal{F}_E=\{O;\hat{e}_x,\hat{e}_y,\hat{e}_z\}$ be the ground inertial frame. Both sides use the same three-degree-of-freedom fixed-wing point-mass model, with position $p=[x,y,z]^\top$, speed $V$, heading $\psi\in(-\pi,\pi]$, and pitch $\gamma\in[-\pi/2,\pi/2]$. Heading is measured in the horizontal plane from $\hat e_x$ toward $\hat e_y$:
\begin{equation}
\dot x = V\cos\gamma\cos\psi,\quad
\dot y = V\cos\gamma\sin\psi,\quad
\dot z = V\sin\gamma,
\label{eq:vel-decomp}
\end{equation}

The offensive and defensive states are
% \begin{align}
% \mathbf s_i^{A}(t)&=\bigl[\,p_i^\top(t),\,V_i(t),\,\psi_i(t),\,\gamma_i(t)\,\bigr]^\top\in\mathbb{R}^{6},
% \label{eq:att-state}\\
% \mathbf s_j^{D}(t)&=\bigl[\,p_j^\top(t),\,V_j(t),\,\psi_j(t),\,\gamma_j(t)\,\bigr]^\top\in\mathbb{R}^{6}.
% \label{eq:def-state}
% \end{align}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf s_i^{A}(t)
=
\bigl[\,p_i^\top(t),\,V_i(t),\,\psi_i(t),\,\gamma_i(t)\,\bigr]^\top
\in\mathbb{R}^{6},
\label{eq:att-state}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\mathbf s_j^{D}(t)
=
\bigl[\,p_j^\top(t),\,V_j(t),\,\psi_j(t),\,\gamma_j(t)\,\bigr]^\top
\in\mathbb{R}^{6}.
\label{eq:def-state}
\end{equation}
Both states use common coordinate and attitude conventions for sensing and guidance.
For HVT position $p_H\in\mathbb{R}^3$, define relative position and range
\begin{equation}
r_{iH}(t)=p_H-p_i(t),\qquad
\rho_{iH}(t)=\|r_{iH}(t)\|.
\label{eq:riH-def}
\end{equation}
and the closing velocity and spatial line-of-sight (LOS) angular-velocity vector
\begin{equation}
V_{c,iH}(t)=\frac{r_{iH}^{\top}(t)v_i(t)}{\rho_{iH}(t)},\qquad
\omega_{iH}^{\mathrm{LOS}}(t)=\frac{r_{iH}(t)\times v_i(t)}{\rho_{iH}^2(t)}.
\label{eq:hvt_kinematics_def}
\end{equation}
These quantities describe terminal approach and transverse LOS rotation. The UAV dynamics are

% \begin{align}
% \dot V_i&=a_{x,i}-g\sin\gamma_i,
% \label{eq:dyn-V}\\
% \dot\psi_i&=\frac{a_{n,\psi,i}}{V_i\cos\gamma_i},
% \label{eq:dyn-psi}\\
% \dot\gamma_i&=\frac{a_{n,\gamma,i}-g\cos\gamma_i}{V_i},
% \label{eq:dyn-gamma}
% \end{align}

\begin{equation}
\dot V_i
=a_{x,i}-g\sin\gamma_i,
\label{eq:dyn-V}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\dot\psi_i
=\frac{a_{n,\psi,i}}{V_i\cos\gamma_i},
\label{eq:dyn-psi}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\dot\gamma_i
=\frac{a_{n,\gamma,i}-g\cos\gamma_i}{V_i},
\label{eq:dyn-gamma}
\end{equation}
where $g$ is gravitational acceleration and $a_{x,i}$, $a_{n,\gamma,i}$, and $a_{n,\psi,i}$ are the axial, pitch-plane normal, and yaw-plane normal controls. Each interceptor obeys the same dynamic structure with corresponding subscript-$j$ controls.

Interceptor $D_j$ has FOV axis $b_j(t)=v_j/\|v_j\|\in\mathbb S^2$ and half-angle $\alpha_j$, with relative geometry
\begin{equation}
r_{ij}(t)=p_i(t)-p_j(t),\qquad
\rho_{ij}(t)=\left\|r_{ij}(t)\right\|.
\end{equation}
Here $r_{ij}$ points from $D_j$ to $A_i$; their body LOS angle is
\begin{equation}
\theta_{ij}^{\mathrm{body}}(t)
=
\arccos
\left(
\frac{b_j^{\mathrm T}(t)r_{ij}(t)}
{\rho_{ij}(t)}
\right).
\end{equation}

The corresponding algebraic FOV margin is
\begin{equation}
q_{ij}(t)
=
\frac{b_j^{\mathrm T}(t)r_{ij}(t)}
{\rho_{ij}(t)}
-\cos\alpha_j ,
\label{eq:qij-def}
\end{equation}
The signs $q_{ij}>0$, $q_{ij}=0$, and $q_{ij}<0$ indicate inside, boundary, and outside cases. Its differentiable $C^1$ occupancy index is
\begin{equation}
s_{ij}(t)
=
\sigma\!\bigl(\kappa_q q_{ij}(t)\bigr),\qquad
\sigma(x)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-x}},
\label{eq:sij-def}
\end{equation}
where $\kappa_q$ controls the sigmoid transition; smoothing-coefficient units make all sigmoid arguments dimensionless.

Let $D_{\mathrm{radar}}$ denote the maximum onboard detection range. Joint range--FOV satisfaction is indicated by
\begin{equation}
\delta_{ij}^{\mathrm{det}}(t)
=
\mathbf{1}
\left\{
\rho_{ij}(t)\le D_{\mathrm{radar}},\
q_{ij}(t)>0
\right\}.
\label{eq:det-indicator}
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:det-indicator} combines range and FOV constraints. Target information is updated at $20\,\mathrm{Hz}$ under onboard detection and at $2\,\mathrm{Hz}$ under ground-radar indication.

The detectable surviving targets of $D_j$ are
\begin{equation}
\mathcal A_{j,\mathrm{in}}(t)
=
\left\{
i\in\mathcal A_{\mathrm{alive}}(t):
\delta_{ij}^{\mathrm{det}}(t)=1
\right\}.
\label{eq:in-detect-set}
\end{equation}
The Hungarian algorithm initializes $m_j(0)=m_j^0$ from ground-radar indication. Onboard takeover selects the nearest detectable survivor, whereas target invalidation selects the nearest survivor. With $\bar m_j=m_j(t^-)$,
\begin{equation}
m_j(t)=
\begin{cases}
\emptyset,
& \mathcal A_{\mathrm{alive}}(t)=\emptyset,\\[1mm]
\displaystyle \arg\min_{i\in \mathcal A_{\mathrm{alive}}(t)}
\rho_{ij}(t),
& \bar m_j\notin \mathcal A_{\mathrm{alive}}(t),\\[3mm]
\displaystyle \arg\min_{i\in \mathcal A_{j,\mathrm{in}}(t)}
\rho_{ij}(t),
& \mathcal A_{j,\mathrm{in}}(t)\neq\emptyset,\\[3mm]
\bar m_j,
& \substack{\bar m_j\in \mathcal A_{\mathrm{alive}}(t),\\
\mathcal A_{j,\mathrm{in}}(t)=\emptyset}.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:fov-lock-rule}
\end{equation}
Here, $m_j(t)\in\mathcal A\cup\{\emptyset\}$ is the current attack target and $m_j^0$ the initial indication assignment. The defender retains $m_j(t)$ internally; it is unavailable to the offensive policy, task-reconfiguration mechanism, and actor observation.

Assigned targets are pursued by proportional navigation:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
a_{n,\gamma,j}^{\mathrm{cmd}}
&=-N_{\mathrm{PN}}\,V_{c,m_j j}\,\dot\lambda_{\mathrm{el},m_j j},\\
a_{n,\psi,j}^{\mathrm{cmd}}
&=-N_{\mathrm{PN}}\,V_{c,m_j j}\,\dot\lambda_{\mathrm{az},m_j j},
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:def-guid-pn}
\end{equation}
Here $a_{n,\gamma,j}^{\mathrm{cmd}}$ and $a_{n,\psi,j}^{\mathrm{cmd}}$ are pitch- and yaw-normal commands, $N_{\mathrm{PN}}$ is the navigation gain, $V_{c,ij}=-r_{ij}^\top(v_i-v_j)/\rho_{ij}$ is closing velocity, and $\dot\lambda_{\mathrm{el},ij}$ and $\dot\lambda_{\mathrm{az},ij}$ are channel LOS angular rates. For $v_{ij}=v_i-v_j$,
\begin{equation}
\omega_{ij}^{\mathrm{LOS}} = \frac{r_{ij}\times v_{ij}}{\rho_{ij}^2},
\label{eq:omega_ij_def}
\end{equation}
Its magnitude is $\|\omega_{ij}^{\mathrm{LOS}}\| = \frac{\|v_{t,ij}\|}{\rho_{ij}}$, with LOS-tangent relative velocity $v_{t,ij}$. An assigned $A_i=m_j(t)$ is destroyed for $\rho_{ij}<\rho^{\mathrm{kill}}$; the HVT is hit for $\rho_{iH}<\rho^{\mathrm{kill}}$.

\section{Safety-Margin Evaluation and Dynamic Task Reconfiguration under Multi-Interceptor Detection Constraints}

\subsection{Zero-Control Prediction-Horizon FOV Margin and Safety-Margin Evaluation for Joint Detection Coverage}

Future detection risk is evaluated through a zero-control prediction-horizon FOV margin. Near the FOV boundary, $q_{ij}$ and $\dot q_{ij}$ describe the local geometric margin and its trend. Assuming no additional active correction over $[t,t+t_{go,ij}(t)]$, constant-rate local motion is represented by
\begin{equation}
\xi_{ij}(t)=
\begin{bmatrix}
q_{ij}(t)\\
\dot q_{ij}(t)
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
\dot \xi_{ij}(t)=A_c\xi_{ij}(t),
\qquad
A_c=
\begin{bmatrix}
0&1\\
0&0
\end{bmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The instantaneous distance rate is
\begin{equation}
\dot\rho_{ij}(t)
=
\frac{r_{ij}^{\top}(t)v_{ij}(t)}{\rho_{ij}(t)}.
\end{equation}
Because $V_{c,ij}(t)=-\dot\rho_{ij}(t)$, the finite prediction time for each UAV--interceptor pair is defined as
\begin{equation}
t_{go,ij}(t)
=
\begin{cases}
\displaystyle
\min\!\left(
\dfrac{\rho_{ij}(t)}{V_{c,ij}(t)},
T_p
\right),
& V_{c,ij}(t)>\varepsilon_v,\\[3mm]
0,
& V_{c,ij}(t)\le\varepsilon_v.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
Here, $\varepsilon_v>0$ prevents excessive near-zero-closing estimates, $T_p>0$ bounds the horizon, and $V_{c,ij}\le\varepsilon_v$ gives no positive prediction; hence $t_{go,ij}$ is finite and is not remaining mission time. With output vector $c=[1,0]^\top$ and state-transition matrix $\Phi_{ij}$, the prediction chain is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
&q_{ij}(t)=c^\top\xi_{ij}(t),\\
&\xi_{ij}\!\left(t+t_{go,ij}(t)\right)
=\Phi_{ij}\!\left(t+t_{go,ij}(t),t\right)\xi_{ij}(t),\\
&q_{ij}\!\left(t+t_{go,ij}(t)\right)
=c^\top\Phi_{ij}\!\left(t+t_{go,ij}(t),t\right)\xi_{ij}(t),\\
&Y_{ij}(t_{go,ij})
=\Phi_{ij}^{\top}\!\left(t+t_{go,ij}(t),t\right)c,\\
&Z_{ij}(t)=Y_{ij}^{\top}(t_{go,ij})\xi_{ij}(t).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
For constant-integrator $A_c$, this gives
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
Y_{ij}(t_{go,ij})
&=
\begin{bmatrix}
1\\
t_{go,ij}
\end{bmatrix},&
Z_{ij}(t)
&=q_{ij}(t)+t_{go,ij}(t)\dot q_{ij}(t).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Thus, $Z_{ij}$ is the predicted FOV margin at the end of the finite zero-control horizon.

Finite onboard range is incorporated through the radial gate and gated single-interceptor margin
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
G_{ij}^{\mathrm{rad}}(t)
&=\sigma\!\left[\kappa_R\left(D_{\mathrm{radar}}-\rho_{ij}(t)\right)\right],\\
Z_{ij}^{\mathrm{det}}(t)
&=G_{ij}^{\mathrm{rad}}(t)\left[Z_{ij}(t)+M_c\right]-M_c,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\kappa_R>0$ is the smoothing coefficient. For $\rho_{ij}\ll D_{\mathrm{radar}}$, $Z_{ij}^{\mathrm{det}}\approx Z_{ij}$; for $\rho_{ij}\gg D_{\mathrm{radar}}$, it approaches $-M_c$ and incurs zero risk penalty.

Multiple interceptor constraints are aggregated by the smooth-max joint detection-risk model
\begin{equation}
\widetilde Z_i(t)
=
\frac{1}{\beta_Z}
\log\left[
\frac{1}{N_I}
\sum_{j\in\mathcal{D}}
\exp\!\bigl(\beta_Z Z_{ij}^{\mathrm{det}}(t)\bigr)
\right],
\end{equation}
where $\beta_Z>0$ controls smoothing, $1/N_I$ removes finite-$\beta_Z$ interceptor-count bias, and $\widetilde Z_i\to\max_{j\in\mathcal D}Z_{ij}^{\mathrm{det}}$ as $\beta_Z\to\infty$. Normalization improves scale comparability.

The swarm safety-margin model is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\Psi_i^{\mathrm{cone}}(t)
&=\max\bigl(0,\, \widetilde Z_i(t)+M_c\bigr),\\
c_t^{\mathrm{cone}}
&=\sum_{i\in\mathcal{A}}\Psi_i^{\mathrm{cone}}(t)
=\sum_{i\in\mathcal{A}}\max\bigl(0,\, \widetilde Z_i(t)+M_c\bigr).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Here, $M_c>0$ is the required margin, $\Psi_i^{\mathrm{cone}}$ vanishes for $\widetilde Z_i\le-M_c$, and $c_t^{\mathrm{cone}}$ aggregates swarm risk.

\subsection{Cooperative Dynamic Task Reconfiguration under an Entropy-Regularized Potential Game}

Because real-time defender allocation is unobservable, the swarm infers capture intention and represents discrete allocation by continuous threat. The normalized range and closing velocity are
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\bar\rho_{ij}(t)
&=\frac{\rho_{ij}(t)}{D_{\mathrm{radar}}},&
\bar V_{c,ij}(t)
&=\operatorname{clip}\!\left(
\frac{V_{c,ij}(t)}{V_{\mathrm{ref}}},
-1,1
\right).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Here $\bar\rho_{ij}$ normalizes range by the detection distance, whereas $\bar V_{c,ij}$ normalizes closing velocity by the reference speed and clips it to a bounded interval.
The dimensionless capture-intent score is
\begin{equation}
\eta_{ij}(t)
=
w_s s_{ij}(t)
-
w_{\rho}\bar\rho_{ij}(t)
+
w_v \bar V_{c,ij}(t),
\end{equation}
where $w_s,w_{\rho},w_v>0$ are dimensionless weights, so both $\eta_{ij}$ and the Softmax exponent are dimensionless. The inferred capture likelihood of $D_j$ for $A_i$ is
\begin{equation}
p_{ij}^{\mathrm{cap}}(t)
=
\frac{\exp\!\bigl(\beta_L \eta_{ij}(t)\bigr)}
{\sum\limits_{k\in\mathcal{A}}\exp\!\bigl(\beta_L \eta_{kj}(t)\bigr)},
\label{eq:soft-lock-prob}
\end{equation}
where dimensionless $\beta_L>0$ controls the concentration of the smooth approximation to discrete capture intention. The integrated threat at the current reconfiguration instant is
\begin{equation}
\lambda_i(t)
=
\sum_{j\in\mathcal D}p_{ij}^{\mathrm{cap}}(t),
\label{eq:soft-lock-pressure}
\end{equation}

The decoy-containment self-cost is
\begin{equation}
C_i^{\mathrm{self}}(t)
=
\sum_{j\in\mathcal{D}}
p_{ij}^{\mathrm{cap}}(t)
\left(
\lambda_E^{\mathrm{self}} E_{ij}(t)
+
\lambda_D D_{ij}(t)
+
\lambda_K K_{ij}(t)
\right),
\end{equation}
where positive $\lambda_E^{\mathrm{self}},\lambda_D,\lambda_K$ weight the capture-likelihood-modulated in-FOV engagement intensity, near-contact cost, and shoot-down risk:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
E_{ij}(t)
&=s_{ij}(t)\exp\!\left(-\frac{\rho_{ij}(t)}{\rho_{\mathrm{ref}}}\right),\\
D_{ij}(t)
&=\sigma\!\bigl(\kappa_d(d_s-\rho_{ij}(t))\bigr),\\
K_{ij}(t)
&=\sigma\!\bigl(\kappa_k(\rho^{\mathrm{kill}}-\rho_{ij}(t))\bigr).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Here $s_{ij}$ is given by \eqref{eq:sij-def}, $\rho_{\mathrm{ref}}$ is the reference distance, $d_s$ is the dangerous-distance threshold, and $\kappa_d,\kappa_k>0$ are smoothing gains. Thus, $E_{ij}$ rises deeper inside the FOV and at shorter range, $D_{ij}$ rises near or below $d_s$, and $K_{ij}$ smoothly approximates the kill-radius threshold.

The attention-transfer benefit when member $i$ draws engagements is
\begin{equation}
B_i^{\mathrm{attn}}(t)
=
\sum_{j\in\mathcal{D}}
\sum_{\substack{k\in\mathcal{A}\\k\neq i}}
p_{ij}^{\mathrm{cap}}(t)\,\Theta_{kj}(t),
\end{equation}
where $\Theta_{kj}$ is interceptor $j$'s threat intensity to UAV $k$:
\begin{equation}
\Theta_{kj}(t)
=
\lambda_Z\,\max\bigl(0,\, Z_{kj}^{\mathrm{det}}(t)+M_c\bigr)
+
\lambda_R \sigma\!\bigl(\kappa_d(d_s-\rho_{kj}(t))\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $d_s$ is the dangerous-distance threshold, and $\lambda_Z,\lambda_R,\kappa_d>0$ are parameters.

The decoy contribution to group penetration is
\begin{equation}
B_i^{H}(t)
=
\sum_{\substack{k\in\mathcal{A}\\k\neq i}}
\lambda_{DH} P_k^{\mathrm{pen}}(t)\,P_k^{\mathrm{hit}}(t),
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_{DH}>0$ is the decoy-task HVT-benefit weight. For member $k$, $P_k^{\mathrm{pen}},P_k^{\mathrm{hit}}\in[0,1]$ are the penetration-efficiency and strike-feasibility metrics used as the swarm-motion prior for online task reconfiguration and defined below.

Equation~\eqref{eq:omega_ij_def} shows that LOS angular-rate magnitude depends strongly on range. During close engagement, decreasing $\rho_{ij}$ nonlinearly amplifies spatial LOS rotation, allowing a short tangential burst maneuver to impose a large angular-rate demand on the interceptor.

Suppose that the offensive UAV applies a maneuver along a favorable direction in the LOS tangent plane within a short time $\Delta t$, and denote the equivalent tangential acceleration as $a_{t,ij}^{\mathrm{cmd}}$. The resulting burst spatial LOS angular velocity can be approximated as
\begin{equation}
\|\omega_{ij}^{\mathrm{LOS},+}(t)\|
\approx
\frac{\|v_{t,ij}(t)+a_{t,ij}^{\mathrm{cmd}}(t)\Delta t\|}{\rho_{ij}(t)}.
\end{equation}
Here, $a_{t,ij}^{\mathrm{cmd}}(t)$ denotes the tangential component of the offensive UAV acceleration command in the LOS tangent plane.
The prerequisite for an interceptor to maintain stable closed-loop tracking is that its achievable maximum tracking angular rate is not lower than the current spatial LOS rotation rate. Because the interceptors are homogeneous, let their maximum normal-acceleration capability be $a_n^{D,\max}$ and let the speed of interceptor $j$ be $V_j$. The maximum trackable LOS angular rate is defined as
\begin{equation}
\omega_{j,\max}^{\mathrm{trk}}(t)
=
\frac{a_n^{D,\max}}{V_j(t)}.
\end{equation}

Based on the above definitions, the local tracking-mismatch margin is constructed as
\begin{equation}
\Gamma_{ij}(t)
=
\|\omega_{ij}^{\mathrm{LOS},+}(t)\|
-
\omega_{j,\max}^{\mathrm{trk}}(t).
\end{equation}
Here, $\Gamma_{ij}(t)>0$ indicates that if a sudden maneuver is triggered at the current time, the LOS angular rate will exceed the maximum trackable capability of the interceptor.

Because this mechanism is significant only under close-range engagement conditions, a near-range gating function is further introduced:
\begin{equation}
G_{ij}^{\mathrm{near}}(t)
=
\sigma\!\bigl(\kappa_{\rho}(\rho_0-\rho_{ij}(t))\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\rho_0$ is the close-range trigger radius and $\kappa_{\rho}>0$ is the smoothing coefficient. The close-range escape trigger of offensive UAV $i$ relative to interceptor $j$ is then defined as
\begin{equation}
\Xi_{ij}(t)
=
G_{ij}^{\mathrm{near}}(t)\,\max\bigl(0,\, \Gamma_{ij}(t)\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\Xi_{ij}(t)$ couples close-range spatial gating with the dynamic mismatch margin. This index produces escape excitation only when the engagement enters the close-range region and the burst maneuver can exceed the tracking limit.

In a multi-interceptor scenario, local escape capability is determined by the most dangerous interceptor. Therefore, the escape-capability index of offensive UAV $i$ is
\begin{equation}
E_i^{\mathrm{esc}}(t)
=
\max_{j\in\mathcal{D}} \Xi_{ij}(t).
\end{equation}

Effective penetration depends on both long-range joint detection risk and close-range escape capability. The penetration-efficiency index is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
P_i^{\mathrm{pen}}(t)
={}&
\sigma\!\bigl(-\kappa_Z(\widetilde Z_i(t)+M_c)\bigr)
\cdot
\sigma\!\bigl(\kappa_d(\rho_i^{\mathrm{threat}}(t)-\rho^{\mathrm{kill}})\bigr)\\
&\cdot
\sigma\!\bigl(\kappa_E E_i^{\mathrm{esc}}(t)\bigr),
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:P_pen_def}
\end{equation}
where $\rho_i^{\mathrm{threat}}(t)=\min_{j\in\mathcal{D}}\rho_{ij}(t)$ is the distance to the most dangerous interceptor, and $\kappa_Z,\kappa_d,\kappa_E>0$ are smoothing coefficients. All factors in \eqref{eq:P_pen_def} use observable relative geometry or onboard flight states; no true defender assignment is used.

The strike-feasibility index $P_i^{\mathrm{hit}}(t)$ is defined as
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
P_i^{\mathrm{hit}}(t)
={}&
\sigma\!\bigl(\kappa_h(\rho^{\mathrm{kill}}-\rho_{iH}(t))\bigr)
\cdot
\sigma\!\bigl(\kappa_c V_{c,iH}(t)\bigr)\\
&\cdot
\sigma\!\bigl(-\kappa_{\omega}\|\omega_{iH}^{\mathrm{LOS}}(t)\|\bigr),
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:P_hit_def}
\end{equation}
where $\kappa_h,\kappa_c,\kappa_{\omega}>0$ smooth the range, closing, and LOS-rate factors; $\kappa_h$ sets the range transition near the strike threshold.

The three task utilities are
\begin{equation}
U_i^{D}(t)
=B_i^{\mathrm{attn}}(t)+B_i^{H}(t)-C_i^{\mathrm{self}}(t),
\label{eq:role-utility-decoy}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
U_i^{P}(t)
=\lambda_{PH}P_i^{\mathrm{pen}}(t)P_i^{\mathrm{hit}}(t)
-\lambda_{PL}\sum_{j\in\mathcal D}p_{ij}^{\mathrm{cap}}(t),
\label{eq:role-utility-pen}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
U_i^{S}(t)
={}&-\lambda_{SZ}\Psi_i^{\mathrm{cone}}(t)
-\lambda_{SL}\sum_{j\in\mathcal D}p_{ij}^{\mathrm{cap}}(t)\\
&-\lambda_{SD}\sigma\!\bigl(\kappa_d(d_s-\rho_i^{\mathrm{threat}}(t))\bigr),
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:role-utility-stealth}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_{PH},\lambda_{PL},\lambda_{SZ},\lambda_{SL},\lambda_{SD}>0$ are weighting coefficients.

Equations~\eqref{eq:role-utility-decoy}--\eqref{eq:role-utility-stealth} associate task $D$ with drawing and containing defensive firepower at acceptable cost, task $P$ with favorable penetration and strike conditions, and task $S$ with concealment, evasion, and switching redundancy.

Without preset division, UAV $i$ uses task-probability vector
\begin{equation}
\mu_i(t)=
\left[
\mu_i^{D}(t),\,
\mu_i^{P}(t),\,
\mu_i^{S}(t)
\right]^{\top},
\end{equation}
whose entries are the current decoy-containment, primary-attack, and concealment-evasion probabilities satisfying
\begin{equation}
\mu_i^{D}(t)+\mu_i^{P}(t)+\mu_i^{S}(t)=1,\qquad
\mu_i^{r}(t)\ge 0,\quad r\in\{D,P,S\}.
\end{equation}
For utilities $U_i^{D},U_i^{P},U_i^{S}$, the Logit response is
\begin{equation}
\mu_i^{r}(t)
=
\frac{\exp\!\left(\dfrac{\eta}{\tau_\mu}U_i^{r}(t)\right)}
{\sum\limits_{s\in\{D,P,S\}}\exp\!\left(\dfrac{\eta}{\tau_\mu}U_i^{s}(t)\right)},
\qquad r\in\{D,P,S\},
\label{eq:role-logit}
\end{equation}
where $\eta>0$ is rationality and $\tau_\mu>0$ entropy weight. At each reconfiguration instant the state is fixed, making $U_i^r(t)$ an instantaneous state-dependent utility.

For this fixed state, the entropy-regularized individual payoff of UAV $i$ is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
J_i(\mu_i,\mu_{-i};t)
={}&
\sum_{r\in\{D,P,S\}}\mu_i^r(t)U_i^r(t)\\
&-\frac{\tau_\mu}{\eta}
\sum_{r\in\{D,P,S\}}\mu_i^r(t)\ln\mu_i^r(t).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The corresponding task potential is
\begin{equation}
\Phi_{\mathrm{task}}(\mu;t)
=
\sum_{i\in\mathcal A}J_i(\mu_i,\mu_{-i};t).
\end{equation}

\noindent\textbf{Proposition 1.} At every task-reconfiguration instant, the mixed-task model is a state-dependent entropy-regularized exact potential game with potential $\Phi_{\mathrm{task}}(\mu;t)$.

\noindent\textit{Proof.} With the current offensive--defensive state fixed, a unilateral change of member $i$'s mixed task strategy does not change the instantaneous payoff terms of members $k\ne i$. Consequently,
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
J_i(\mu_i',\mu_{-i};t)-J_i(\mu_i,\mu_{-i};t)
={}&\Phi_{\mathrm{task}}(\mu_i',\mu_{-i};t)\\
&-\Phi_{\mathrm{task}}(\mu_i,\mu_{-i};t),
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
which is the defining unilateral-deviation identity of an exact potential game. For the constraint $\sum_r\mu_i^r=1$, introduce a Lagrange multiplier $\varrho_i$. Stationarity of
\begin{equation}
J_i+\varrho_i\left(1-\sum_r\mu_i^r\right)
\label{eq:task-game-lagrangian}
\end{equation}
gives
\begin{equation}
U_i^r-\frac{\tau_\mu}{\eta}\left(\ln\mu_i^r+1\right)-\varrho_i=0.
\label{eq:task-game-stationarity}
\end{equation}
and hence $\mu_i^r\propto\exp[(\eta/\tau_\mu)U_i^r]$. Normalization recovers \eqref{eq:role-logit}. \hfill$\square$


The cooperative penetration problem can therefore be characterized as the following state-dependent entropy-regularized exact potential game:
\begin{equation}
\mu^{\star}(t)
=
\arg\max_{\{\mu_i\}_{i\in\mathcal{A}}}
\Phi_{\mathrm{task}}(\mu;t).
\end{equation}
This state-driven mechanism reconfigures cooperative tasks online without preset allocation, using local adversarial cost, group penetration benefit, and defensive-attention redirection.

\section{Cooperative UAV-Swarm Penetration Value Evaluation and Intelligent Guidance Policy Optimization}

Because individual and group optima differ, this section balances effective penetration against losses and embeds the resulting indicators into multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) under centralized training and decentralized execution (CTDE).

\subsection{Utility Functional for Cooperative UAV-Swarm Penetration}

The value model evaluates effective penetration and avoidable loss under multi-interceptor engagement.

Using \eqref{eq:P_pen_def} and \eqref{eq:P_hit_def}, define the individual contribution and continuous effective-UAV count as
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
e_i(t)
&=P_i^{\mathrm{pen}}(t)P_i^{\mathrm{hit}}(t),\\
N_{\mathrm{eff}}(t)
&=\sum_{i\in\mathcal A}e_i(t)
=\sum_{i\in\mathcal A}P_i^{\mathrm{pen}}(t)P_i^{\mathrm{hit}}(t).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
At $T$, $N_{\mathrm{eff}}$ represents surviving, strike-capable penetrators.

Let $\ell_i(T)\in\{0,1\}$ indicate whether UAV $i$ is destroyed before the terminal time, and define its destruction time as
\begin{equation}
t_i^{\mathrm{kill}}
=
\inf\left\{
t\in[0,T]:
i\notin\mathcal A_{\mathrm{alive}}(t)
\right\}.
\end{equation}
At the last valid pre-destruction instant $t_i^{\mathrm{kill}-}$, the UAV is considered salvageable when detection risk is low, interceptor separation is sufficient, and escape capability is positive:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\chi_i^{\mathrm{salvage}}\!\left(t_i^{\mathrm{kill}-}\right)
={}&
\sigma\!\left[-\kappa_Z\left(\widetilde Z_i\!\left(t_i^{\mathrm{kill}-}\right)+M_c\right)\right]\\
&\cdot
\sigma\!\left[\kappa_d\left(\rho_i^{\mathrm{threat}}\!\left(t_i^{\mathrm{kill}-}\right)-\rho_s\right)\right]\\
&\cdot
\sigma\!\left[\kappa_E E_i^{\mathrm{esc}}\!\left(t_i^{\mathrm{kill}-}\right)\right],
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\rho_s>0$ is the recoverable-separation threshold. Ineffective sacrifice is
\begin{equation}
U_i^{\mathrm{waste}}(T)
=
\begin{cases}
\chi_i^{\mathrm{salvage}}\!\left(t_i^{\mathrm{kill}-}\right),
&
\ell_i(T)=1,\\[2mm]
0,
&
\ell_i(T)=0.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
Only destroyed UAVs require $t_i^{\mathrm{kill}-}$; survivors contribute zero. Thus,
\begin{equation}
N_{\mathrm{waste}}(T)
=
\sum_{i\in\mathcal{A}} U_i^{\mathrm{waste}}(T).
\end{equation}

Simultaneous arrivals produce the saturation-strike efficiency $J_{\mathrm{sat}}(T)=\lambda_1N_{\mathrm{eff}}(T)+\lambda_2N_{\mathrm{eff}}^2(T)$, whose quadratic term promotes coordinated terminal entry.

Let $N_{\mathrm{loss}}(T)=\sum_{i\in\mathcal{A}}\ell_i(T)$ be the total loss number over the complete penetration process. The terminal total value function is defined as
\begin{equation}
J_T
=
J_{\mathrm{sat}}(T)
-
\lambda_L N_{\mathrm{loss}}(T)
-
\lambda_U N_{\mathrm{waste}}(T),
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_L,\lambda_U>0$ penalize total and ineffective losses; sacrifice is valuable only if it enables other penetrators.

Define the running utility
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
L(t)
={}&
\lambda_P \sum_{i\in\mathcal{A}} P_i^{\mathrm{pen}}(t)
+
\lambda_{JH} \sum_{i\in\mathcal{A}} P_i^{\mathrm{hit}}(t)\\
&
-
\lambda_C \sum_{i\in\mathcal{A}} \Psi_i^{\mathrm{cone}}(t)
-
\lambda_S \sum_{i\in\mathcal{A}} C_i^{\mathrm{self}}(t).
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:running-utility}
\end{equation}
The operation-level objective is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal J
=
\mathbb E\!\left[
\int_0^T L(t)\,dt+J_T
\right].
\end{equation}
Here $\Psi_i^{\mathrm{cone}}$ and $C_i^{\mathrm{self}}$ are detection-risk and defensive-threat costs; positive $\lambda_P,\lambda_{JH},\lambda_C,\lambda_S$ balance them against penetration and strike terms.

\subsection{Markov Decision Modeling Based on Theoretical Priors}
\label{subsec:rl-design}

Based on the preceding theoretical quantities, each offensive UAV is modeled as a policy agent with policy
\begin{equation}
\pi_{\theta_i}\bigl(u_i(t)\,\big|\,\bar o_i(t)\bigr),\qquad i\in\mathcal{A},
\end{equation}
where $\bar o_i(t)$ and $u_i(t)$ are the enhanced observation and normalized action.

The continuous action is
\begin{equation}
u_i(t)=\bigl[u_i^{(1)}(t),\,u_i^{(2)}(t),\,u_i^{(3)}(t)\bigr]^{\top}\in[-1,1]^3,
\label{eq:rl-action}
\end{equation}
whose components command axial, pitch-normal, and yaw-normal maneuvers. A biased affine mapping converts them to the accelerations in \eqref{eq:dyn-V}--\eqref{eq:dyn-gamma}:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
a_{x,i}
&=
\begin{cases}
u_i^{(1)}(0-a_{x,\min}), & u_i^{(1)}\le0,\\
u_i^{(1)}(a_{x,\max}-0), & u_i^{(1)}>0,
\end{cases}\\
a_{n,\gamma,i}
&=
\begin{cases}
g+u_i^{(2)}\,\bigl(a_n^{A,\max}-g\bigr), & u_i^{(2)}\ge 0,\\[2pt]
g+u_i^{(2)}\,\bigl(a_n^{A,\max}+g\bigr), & u_i^{(2)}<0,
\end{cases}
\\
a_{n,\psi,i}
&=u_i^{(3)}\,a_n^{A,\max}.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:act-map}
\end{equation}
Here $a_{x,\min},a_{x,\max}$ bound axial acceleration and $a_n^{A,\max}$ bounds normal acceleration. For $u_i=\mathbf0$, \eqref{eq:act-map} gives $a_{x,i}=0$, $a_{n,\gamma,i}=g$, and $a_{n,\psi,i}=0$; with $\gamma_i=0$, this is level, constant-speed, straight-flight trim.

The local observation $o_i(t)\in\mathbb R^{11+4K}$ and task-enhanced actor input are
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
o_i(t)
&=\bigl[\,o_i^{\mathrm{self}}(t)\mid o_i^{H}(t)\mid
o_i^{\mathrm{thr}}(t)\mid o_i^{\mathrm{team}}(t)\,\bigr]^\top,\\
\bar o_i(t)
&=\left[o_i^\top(t),\mu_i^\top(t)\right]^\top
\in\mathbb R^{14+4K}.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:obs-decomp}
\end{equation}
The HVT relative-motion components are
\begin{align}
\dot\lambda_{\mathrm{az},iH}(t)&=\frac{r_{iH,x}\,\dot r_{iH,y}-r_{iH,y}\,\dot r_{iH,x}}{r_{iH,x}^{2}+r_{iH,y}^{2}},
\label{eq:los-az}\\
\dot\lambda_{\mathrm{el},iH}(t)&=\frac{\dot r_{iH,z}\bigl(r_{iH,x}^{2}+r_{iH,y}^{2}\bigr)
-r_{iH,z}\bigl(r_{iH,x}\dot r_{iH,x}+r_{iH,y}\dot r_{iH,y}\bigr)}{\rho_{iH}^{2}\,\sqrt{r_{iH,x}^{2}+r_{iH,y}^{2}}},
\label{eq:los-el}\\
V_{c,iH}(t)&=\frac{r_{iH}^{\top}v_i}{\rho_{iH}},
\label{eq:vc-h}\\
c_{\mathrm{align},iH}(t)&=\frac{v_i^{\top}r_{iH}}{\|v_i\|\,\rho_{iH}},
\label{eq:cos-align}
\end{align}
where $c_{\mathrm{align},iH}$ is distance-independent heading alignment. Threat normalization uses
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\widetilde V_{c,ij}(t)
&=\operatorname{clip}\!\left(\frac{V_{c,ij}(t)}{V_{\mathrm{ref}}},-1,1\right),\\
\widetilde\Gamma_{ij}(t)
&=\operatorname{clip}\!\left(\frac{\Gamma_{ij}(t)}{\omega_{\mathrm{ref}}},-1,1\right).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Because $\Gamma_{ij}$ has LOS-rate units, $\omega_{\mathrm{ref}}$ normalizes it. The $K=3$ largest-$p_{ij}^{\mathrm{cap}}$ interceptors are ordered by
\begin{equation}
p_{i j_i^{(1)}}^{\mathrm{cap}}(t)
\ge p_{i j_i^{(2)}}^{\mathrm{cap}}(t)
\ge\cdots\ge
p_{i j_i^{(K)}}^{\mathrm{cap}}(t).
\end{equation}
The observation components are summarized compactly below; $V_{\mathrm{ref}}$, $a^{\mathrm{ref}}_n$, and $\omega_{\mathrm{ref}}$ are normalization constants.

\label{tab:actor-observation-components}

\(\begin{aligned}
o_i^{\mathrm{self}}(t)=[&V_i/V_{\mathrm{ref}},\sin\gamma_i,\cos\gamma_i,\\

Fewer than $K$ valid threat entries are zero padded, and none reveals true defender assignment. The online task prior augments the actor input and is also available to the centralized critic during training.

Individual and shared running rewards plus a terminal term begin with range, closing, and retreat shaping:
\begin{equation}
r_i^{\mathrm{app}}(t)=\lambda_{\mathrm{app}}\,\frac{\rho_{iH}(t-\Delta t)-\rho_{iH}(t)}{\rho_{\mathrm{ref}}},
\label{eq:r-approach}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
r_i^{\mathrm{cls}}(t)=\lambda_{c}\,\frac{V_{c,iH}(t)}{V_{\mathrm{ref}}},
\label{eq:r-closing}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
r_i^{\mathrm{ret}}(t)=-\lambda_r\,\frac{\max\bigl(-V_{c,iH}(t),\,0\bigr)}{V_{\mathrm{ref}}}.
\label{eq:r-retreat}
\end{equation}
Equations~\eqref{eq:r-approach} and \eqref{eq:r-closing} reward approach; \eqref{eq:r-retreat} penalizes retreat.

LOS pointing uses $c_{\mathrm{align},iH}$ and vertical alignment
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
c_{\gamma,iH}(t)
&=\cos\!\bigl(\gamma_i(t)-\gamma_{iH}^{\mathrm{des}}(t)\bigr),\\
\gamma_{iH}^{\mathrm{des}}(t)
&=\arctan\!\frac{r_{iH,z}(t)}{\sqrt{r_{iH,x}^{2}+r_{iH,y}^{2}}},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
to construct
\begin{equation}
r_i^{\mathrm{algn}}(t)=\lambda_h\,c_{\mathrm{align},iH}(t)+\lambda_g\,c_{\gamma,iH}(t),
\label{eq:r-align}
\end{equation}
and a quadratic heading penalty:
\begin{equation}
r_i^{\mathrm{herr}}(t)=-\lambda_e\,\Bigl(\dfrac{\arccos c_{\mathrm{align},iH}(t)}{\pi}\Bigr)^{2}.
\label{eq:r-herr}
\end{equation}

For $\rho_{\min}(t)=\min_{i\in\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{alive}}(t)}\rho_{iH}(t)$, shared progress is
\begin{equation}
r_t^{\mathrm{tmin}}(t)=\lambda_{\mathrm{tm}}\,\frac{\rho_{\min}(t-\Delta t)-\rho_{\min}(t)}{\rho_{\mathrm{ref}}}.
\label{eq:r-team}
\end{equation}

The theoretical utility reward is the forward-Euler discretization of the running term in $\mathcal J$:
\begin{equation}
r_t^{\mathrm{util}}(t)
=
\Delta t\,L(t).
\label{eq:r-prior}
\end{equation}
The progress, escape, and feasibility terms are auxiliary shaping rewards, while $r^T$ is the discrete counterpart of $J_T$. Escape shaping is
\begin{equation}
r_t^{\mathrm{esc}}(t)=\lambda_{\mathrm{esc}}\sum_{i\in\mathcal{A}}E_i^{\mathrm{esc}}(t).
\end{equation}
The reward weights $\lambda_P$, $\lambda_{JH}$, $\lambda_C$, $\lambda_S$, and $\lambda_{\mathrm{esc}}$ are distinct from task-utility and self-containment weights.

Feasibility uses boundary, terrain, range, and yaw penalties:
\begin{equation}
\widetilde\rho_{iH}(t)
=
1-
\exp\!\left(-\frac{\rho_{iH}(t)}{\rho_{\mathrm{ref}}}\right).
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
r_i^{\mathrm{bnd}}(t)=-\lambda_b\,\mathbb{I}_{\mathrm{out}}(p_i(t)),
\label{eq:r-bnd}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
r_i^{\mathrm{grd}}(t)=-\lambda_{\mathrm{gr}}\,\bigl(\max(0,\,1-z_i(t)/z^{\mathrm{safe}})\bigr)^{2},
\label{eq:r-grd}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
r_i^{\mathrm{prx}}(t)=-\lambda_{\mathrm{px}}\,\frac{\rho_{iH}(t)}{\rho_{\mathrm{ref}}},
\label{eq:r-prx}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
r_i^{\mathrm{reg}}(t)=-\lambda_{\mu}\,\bigl|u_i^{(3)}(t)\bigr|\cdot \widetilde\rho_{iH}(t).
\label{eq:r-reg}
\end{equation}
As $\widetilde\rho_{iH}$ decreases near the HVT, yaw regularization relaxes. Sparse events use
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
r_i^{\mathrm{hit}}(t)
&=\lambda_{\mathrm{hit}}\mathbb{I}\bigl[\,\rho_{iH}(t)<\rho^{\mathrm{kill}}\,\bigr],\\
r_i^{\mathrm{kld}}(t)
&=-\lambda_{\mathrm{kl}}\,\mathbb{I}_{\text{just-killed}}(i,t),
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:r-event}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_{\mathrm{hit}}$ rewards hits, $\lambda_{\mathrm{kl}}$ penalizes losses, and $r_i^{\mathrm{stp}}=-\lambda_s$ promotes completion.

The individual, team, and total running rewards are
\begin{align}
r_i^{\mathrm{ind}}(t)
&=r_i^{\mathrm{app}}+r_i^{\mathrm{cls}}+r_i^{\mathrm{ret}}
+r_i^{\mathrm{algn}}+r_i^{\mathrm{herr}}\notag\\
&\quad+r_i^{\mathrm{bnd}}+r_i^{\mathrm{grd}}+r_i^{\mathrm{prx}}+r_i^{\mathrm{reg}}
+r_i^{\mathrm{hit}}+r_i^{\mathrm{kld}}+r_i^{\mathrm{stp}},
\label{eq:r-ind}\\
r_t^{\mathrm{team}}(t)
&=r_t^{\mathrm{tmin}}(t)+r_t^{\mathrm{util}}(t)+r_t^{\mathrm{esc}}(t),\\
R(t)
&=\sum_{i\in\mathcal A}r_i^{\mathrm{ind}}(t)+r_t^{\mathrm{team}}(t).
\label{eq:r-total}
\end{align}

The terminal reward is
\begin{equation}
r^{T}=J_T,
\label{eq:r-term}
\end{equation}
which retains the effective-penetration, simultaneous-entry, total-loss, and ineffective-sacrifice terms defined above.

The training objective is to maximize the discounted cumulative reward under the behavior policy used to collect trajectories:
\begin{equation}
J(\theta)=\mathbb{E}_{b_\theta}\Biggl[\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\gamma^{t} R(t)\;+\;\gamma^{T} r^{T}\Biggr],
\label{eq:rl-objective}
\end{equation}
where $\gamma\in(0,1)$ is the discount factor. The CTDE paradigm is adopted in the multi-agent execution framework: during training, the critic network can access the global state $s(t)=\bigl(\{\mathbf s_i^A\},\{\mathbf s_j^D\},p_H\bigr)$, whereas during execution each $\pi_{\theta_i}$ generates its maneuver command from the enhanced actor observation $\bar o_i(t)$.

\subsection{Task-Trust Attention Cooperative Penetration Policy Optimization Algorithm}
\label{subsubsec:rta-mappo}

TTA-MAPPO augments MAPPO with dynamic task priors, trust-modulated exploration, and centralized-critic attention for cooperative penetration under multi-interceptor FOV constraints. In TTA-MAPPO, ``task'' refers to the dynamically reconfigured task-probability prior, ``trust'' denotes return-adaptive exploration regulation, and ``attention'' refers to the cross-agent attention used by the centralized critic. Figure~\ref{fig:algorithm-framework} shows its centralized-evaluation, distributed-execution architecture, whose actor factorization is
\begin{equation}
\pi_\theta(\mathbf u\mid\bar{\mathbf o})
=
\prod_{i\in\mathcal A}
\pi_{\theta_i}(u_i\mid\bar o_i),
\end{equation}
where $\bar o_i(t)$ is the actor input available to UAV $i$ during execution, $u_i(t)$ is its maneuver action, and $\bar{\mathbf o}(t)$ and $\mathbf u(t)$ are the joint enhanced observation and joint action, respectively. No actor directly processes the raw observation or encoded neural feature of another UAV $k\ne i$ during execution.

\begin{figure*}[!t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/algorithm_framework.pdf}
  \caption{Schematic of the cooperative penetration strategy for the UAV swarm}
  \label{fig:algorithm-framework}
\end{figure*}

During centralized training, the critic uses global state and theoretical priors:
\begin{equation}
V_{\phi}(t)
=
V_{\phi}\bigl(s(t),\zeta(t)\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $s(t)$ is the centralized global state and $\zeta(t)$ is a structured prior composed of detection risk, effective penetration value, and dynamic task-reconfiguration results:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\zeta(t)
=
\Bigl[
\widetilde Z_1,\cdots,\widetilde Z_{N_A},
P_1^{\mathrm{pen}},\cdots,P_{N_A}^{\mathrm{pen}},\\
P_1^{\mathrm{hit}},\cdots,P_{N_A}^{\mathrm{hit}},
\mu_1,\cdots,\mu_{N_A}
\Bigr],
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\widetilde Z_i$, $P_i^{\mathrm{pen}}$, $P_i^{\mathrm{hit}}$, and $\mu_i$ are aggregate risk, penetration efficiency, strike feasibility, and reconfigured task distribution.

The actor uses a residual encoder to process its enhanced observation:
\begin{equation}
h_i^{A,0}=f_{\mathrm{enc}}^{A}\bigl(\bar o_i(t)\bigr),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
h_i^{A}
=
h_i^{A,0}+f_{\mathrm{res}}^{A}\bigl(h_i^{A,0}\bigr).
\end{equation}
This residual MLP encodes $\bar o_i$ and parameterizes the mean and covariance of the latent Gaussian actor.
Cross-agent attention is used only by the centralized critic during training. For the $i$th global feature $\chi_i(t)$ extracted from $s(t)$ and $\zeta(t)$,
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
g_i^{C,0}
&=f_{\mathrm{enc}}^{C}\bigl(\chi_i(t)\bigr),\\
\alpha_{ik}^{C}
&=
\frac{
\exp\left[(W_qg_i^{C,0})^{\top}(W_kg_k^{C,0})/\sqrt d\right]
}{
\sum_{\ell\in\mathcal A}
\exp\left[(W_qg_i^{C,0})^{\top}(W_kg_\ell^{C,0})/\sqrt d\right]
},\\
\bar g_i^{C}
&=\sum_{k\in\mathcal A}\alpha_{ik}^{C}W_vg_k^{C,0}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
g_i^{C}
=
g_i^{C,0}
+f_{\mathrm{res}}^{C}\!\left([g_i^{C,0},\bar g_i^{C}]\right).
\end{equation}
Here, $W_q,W_k,W_v$ generate query, key, and value features, and $d$ is the key dimension. The residual $g_i^C$ serves centralized value estimation only; actors process $\bar o_i$ during decentralized execution.

To address low exploration efficiency, trust-modulated exploration linearly normalizes the moving-average episodic return to $[0,1]$ as $\bar R_i(t)$. The trust factor is
\begin{equation}
\eta_i(t)
=
\mathrm{clip}
\left(
\frac{\bar R_i(t)-R_{\min}}{R_{\max}-R_{\min}},
0,1
\right),
\end{equation}
where $R_{\min}=0$ and $R_{\max}=1$ are its bounds. Exploration intensity is
\begin{equation}
\epsilon_i(t)
=
\epsilon_{\min}
+
\bigl(\epsilon_{\max}-\epsilon_{\min}\bigr)
\bigl(1-\eta_i(t)\bigr).
\end{equation}

The actor and task-guided mixture generate latent action $\widetilde u_i(t)\in\mathbb R^3$; componentwise squashing gives
\begin{equation}
u_i(t)
=
\tanh\!\left(\widetilde u_i(t)\right),
\end{equation}
which guarantees $u_i(t)\in(-1,1)^3\subset[-1,1]^3$. The Gaussian $\widetilde\pi_{\theta_i}(\widetilde u_i\mid\bar o_i)$ is the latent density, and the same tanh transformation and change-of-variables rule induce the bounded executed density $\pi_{\theta_i}(u_i\mid\bar o_i)$. The latent behavior policy is
\begin{equation}
\widetilde b_{\theta_i}(\widetilde u_i\mid\bar o_i,\mu_i)
=
(1-\epsilon_i)\widetilde\pi_{\theta_i}(\widetilde u_i\mid\bar o_i)
+\epsilon_i\widetilde\pi_i^{\mathrm{exp}}(\widetilde u_i\mid o_i,\mu_i).
\end{equation}
The joint executed-action behavior policy is
\begin{equation}
b_\theta(\mathbf u\mid\bar{\mathbf o},\mu)
=
\prod_{i\in\mathcal A}b_{\theta_i}(u_i\mid\bar o_i,\mu_i),
\end{equation}
where $\mu(t)=\bigl(\mu_1(t),\ldots,\mu_{N_A}(t)\bigr)$ collects the task-probability vectors used when the joint action is sampled. The executed-action density $b_{\theta_i}$ is obtained from the latent density $\widetilde b_{\theta_i}$ through the standard change-of-variables rule:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\log b_{\theta_i}(u_i\mid\cdot)
= {}&
\log
\widetilde b_{\theta_i}
\left(
\operatorname{arctanh}(u_i)\mid\cdot
\right)
\\
&-
\sum_{\ell=1}^{3}
\log
\left[
1-\left(u_i^{(\ell)}\right)^2+\epsilon_{\mathrm{num}}
\right],
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon_{\mathrm{num}}=10^{-6}$ avoids numerical singularities. The same transformation relates the latent actor density $\widetilde\pi_{\theta_i}$ to the executed actor density $\pi_{\theta_i}$. During training, actions are sampled from the task-guided latent behavior policy $\widetilde b_{\theta_i}$ and then squashed:
\begin{equation}
\widetilde u_i(t)
\sim
\widetilde b_{\theta_i}
\bigl(\cdot\mid\bar o_i(t),\mu_i(t)\bigr),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
u_i(t)
=
\tanh\!\left(\widetilde u_i(t)\right).
\end{equation}

The exploration policy $\widetilde\pi_i^{\mathrm{exp}}$ is a task-weighted latent Gaussian mixture:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\widetilde\pi_i^{\mathrm{exp}}(\widetilde u_i\mid o_i,\mu_i)
={}&
\mu_i^D(t)\,\mathcal{N}\!\left(\widetilde u_i;u_i^{D,\mathrm{ref}},\,\Sigma\right)\\
&+
\mu_i^P(t)\,\mathcal{N}\!\left(\widetilde u_i;u_i^{P,\mathrm{ref}},\,\Sigma\right)\\
&+
\mu_i^S(t)\,\mathcal{N}\!\left(\widetilde u_i;u_i^{S,\mathrm{ref}},\,\Sigma\right)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\Sigma$ is the base exploration covariance matrix. The vectors $u_i^{r,\mathrm{ref}}$, $r\in\{D,P,S\}$, are heuristic reference-action biases for the three tasks.

The deterministic, saturated reference mappings are
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
j^\star
&=\arg\max_{j\in\mathcal D}p_{ij}^{\mathrm{cap}}(t),\\
u_i^{P,\mathrm{ref}}(t)
&=\mathcal G_P\bigl(o_i^H(t)\bigr),\\
u_i^{D,\mathrm{ref}}(t)
&=\mathcal G_D\bigl(o_{i,j^\star}^{\mathrm{thr}}(t)\bigr),\\
u_i^{S,\mathrm{ref}}(t)
&=\mathcal G_S\!\left(\{o_{i,j}^{\mathrm{thr}}(t)\}_{j\in\mathcal D}\right).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Here $j^\star$ is inferred from capture likelihood rather than true assignment. The maps respectively reduce HVT pointing error, increase the critical interceptor's LOS-tracking burden, and move away from dominant joint detection risk. They only set exploration-Gaussian means: no nonlinear optimization is solved online, and the learned, task-modulated policy still generates the final action.

The PPO clipped objective is used for policy update:
\begin{equation}
J_{\mathrm{clip}}(\theta)
=
\mathbb E_t
\left[
\min
\left(
r_t(\theta)\hat A_t,
\mathrm{clip}\bigl(r_t(\theta),1-\varepsilon,1+\varepsilon\bigr)\hat A_t
\right)
\right],
\end{equation}
The advantage estimate $\hat A_t$ is computed using generalized advantage estimation with $\lambda_{\mathrm{GAE}}=0.95$.
\begin{equation}
r_t(\theta)
=
\frac{
b_{\theta}\bigl(\mathbf u(t)\mid \bar{\mathbf o}(t),\mu(t)\bigr)
}{
b_{\theta_{\mathrm{old}}}\bigl(\mathbf u(t)\mid \bar{\mathbf o}(t),\mu(t)\bigr)
}.
\end{equation}
The denominator is evaluated using the old network parameters and the corresponding exploration intensities stored when the trajectory was sampled. Both the numerator and denominator are executed-action densities after tanh squashing and Jacobian correction.
The entropy term of the squashed behavior policy is estimated from the sampled action log densities in each mini-batch.

For deterministic evaluation and all reported HIL tests, exploration is disabled and the latent actor mean is squashed:
\begin{equation}
\epsilon_i^{\mathrm{eval}}=0,
\qquad
u_i^{\mathrm{eval}}(t)
=
\tanh
\left[
\mu_{\theta_i}
\left(
\bar o_i(t)
\right)
\right],
\end{equation}
where $\mu_{\theta_i}(\bar o_i)$ is the mean of $\widetilde\pi_{\theta_i}$; thus the mixed behavior policy collects training data, whereas the learned actor produces reported execution.

The loss function of the centralized critic network is
\begin{equation}
L_V(\phi)
=
\mathbb E_t
\left[
\left(
V_{\phi}(s(t),\zeta(t))
-
\hat R_t
\right)^2
\right].
\end{equation}

Finally, the joint optimization objective of TTA-MAPPO is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal L(\theta,\phi)
=
-J_{\mathrm{clip}}(\theta)
+
c_v L_V(\phi)
-
c_e\mathcal H(b_{\theta}),
\end{equation}
where $c_v$, $c_e$, and $\mathcal H(b_{\theta})$ are the value-loss weight, entropy weight, and behavior entropy. Hence $b_\theta$ and the critic are training-only, whereas $\pi_\theta$ maps each $\bar o_i$ to deterministic squashed-mean actions for HIL evaluation and execution.

The second removes the task-probability vector $\mu_i=[\mu_i^D,\mu_i^P,\mu_i^S]^{\top}$, thereby eliminating the dynamic task-reconfiguration prior from the actor input while retaining all other observations. Each configuration uses $1000$ HIL Monte Carlo episodes; Table~\ref{tab:ablation-rta-prior} reports the penetration success rates.

These channels support threat-geometry recognition and tracking-mismatch assessment. Masking $\mu_i$ limits every scale to at most $1.3\%$, identifying the explicit task prior as the key channel for online task reconfiguration in actor decisions.

In summary, stable cooperation emerges across scales: decoy-containment disrupts interceptor assignment, while decoy-containment and concealment-evasion protect primary-attack channels. The three tasks form and switch online with the engagement state, demonstrating scale adaptability.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper develops a dynamic-task-reconfiguration-based intelligent guidance method for cooperative penetration by UAV swarms under swarm interception. The principal conclusions are as follows:


1) The zero-control prediction-horizon FOV margin and the joint safety-margin model characterize the detection risk to offensive UAVs under overlapping multi-interceptor FOV coverage in complex engagement geometries, providing the risk basis for online task reconfiguration.

2) The entropy-regularized potential-game mechanism dynamically reconfigures cooperative tasks without preset assignments. As the engagement geometry evolves, it updates the task-probability distributions of individual UAVs online, enabling the offensive swarm to form decoy-containment, concealment-evasion, and primary-attack behaviors.

3) The TTA-MAPPO method incorporates the dynamic-task-reconfiguration prior into both centralized value estimation and task-conditioned decentralized policy execution, enabling the reconfigured task probabilities to guide cooperative maneuver decisions while improving guidance-policy convergence in a high-dimensional continuous action space.

4) The hardware-in-the-loop simulations show stable cooperative penetration behavior and adaptive task switching in response to the evolving interception geometry. Decoy maneuvers repeatedly disrupt interceptor--target assignments, thereby preserving viable penetration corridors for primary-attack UAVs to strike the HVT.
